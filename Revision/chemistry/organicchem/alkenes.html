Alkenes

Cracking Hydrocarbons

Longer-chain hydrocarbons can be broken down into shorter hydrocarbons (which are more useful) through a process called cracking.

During thermal cracking:
-The hydrocarbons are heated until they vapourise.
-The vapour is passed over a hot catalyst.
-A thermal decomposition reaction then takes place.
-The products include alkanes and alkenes.

During steam cracking, hydrocarbons are mixed with steam and heated to a high temperature. Some of the products of cracking are useful as fuels. There is a high demand for fuels with small chains of carbon atoms because they are easy to ignite and have low boiling points. Alkenes can be used to make a range of new compounds, including polymers and industrial alcohol.

Alkenes

Unlike alkanes, alkenes can have double bonds between their carbon atoms as well as single bonds. Since they have a double bond, alkenes are described as unsaturated.
The general formula for alkenes is:

The simplest alkene is ethene, which has the formula: C2H4
It is made up of four hydrogen atoms and two carbon atoms that share a double bond: (C=C).

Alkenes can be drawn using displayed formula:

(Ethene, C2H4)

Reactions of Alkenes

Alkenes are more reactive than alkanes due to the double carbon bonds. Alkenes can combust with oxygen although they tend to produce more smoke than alkanes due to incomplete combustion.
Hydrogen can be added to alkenes to produce alkanes. A nickel catalyst is used for the reaction:

Ethene + Hydrogen -> Ethane

This is an addition reaction.

Ethanol can be produced by reacting ethene with steam in the presence of a catalyst, phosphoric acid:

Ethene + Steam -> Ethanol (C2H5OH)

Bromine Water

As alkenes are more reactive than alkanes, they can react with bromine water. This reaction can be used to differentiate between alkenes and alkanes:

Ethene (colourless) + Bromine Water (orange brown) -> Colourless Solution (Alkenes)

Ethane (colourless) + Bromine Water (orange brown) -> Orange Brown Solution (Alkanes)
